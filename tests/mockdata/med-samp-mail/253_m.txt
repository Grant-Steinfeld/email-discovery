I=E2=80=99ve always found it a bit funny that the term =E2=80=9Cpromoter=
=E2=80=9D gets barely=2C if ever at all=2C used in Silicon Valley. It=E2=
=80=99s simply not part of the vocabulary=2C the way it was commonly used=

=2C have a very important job: they create awareness and interest for some=
thing new=2C and then use that awareness to sell tickets=2C or stocks=2C o=
r whatever=2C to an eagerly buying audience. Their job is to make sure tha=
t the retail public is made aware that they simply have to go attend this=
 nearly sold-out concert from a hot new band=2C or buy some oversubscribed=
 shares of a hot new stock. Their job is to create demand=2C and particula=
rly to do so in an environment where buyers will be highly influenced by w=
hat they believe other buyers are thinking. To put it in last week=E2=80=
=99s terms=2C their is to create a positive feedback cycle of mimicry: not=
hing sells tickets better than
buyers realizing they=E2=80=99re selling out. The same goes for stocks=2C=
 perhaps more so.

The interesting part of the promoter=E2=80=99s job=2C and which often lead=
s to bad behavior=2C is the fact that most of the time the promoter will h=
ave more information than the buyers do as to whether the product they=E2=
=80=99re hyping up is actually any good or not. If the band they=E2=80=99r=
e promoting or the stock they=E2=80=99re pumping is actually great but peo=
ple don=E2=80=99t know about it - so=2C if [perceived value < actual valu=
e] - then the promoter=E2=80=99s job is to create hype - if they fail=2C t=
hen they=E2=80=99re stuck with a bunch of unsold product. But in the oppos=
ite situation=2C where the band actually stinks=2C and [perceived value >=
 actual value]=2C the promoter=E2=80=99s job is still to create hype=2C pe=
rhaps even more so - they need to make sure they can dump all their produc=
t onto unsuspecting buyers before the crowd gets wise.

As you=E2=80=99d expect=2C stock promoters have a long history of involvem=
ent in bubbles=2C scams and unscrupulous behavior. But they also have play=

ve fevers that unlock cheap capital and direct it into the hands of entrep=

=2E By creating irrational exuberance and inflated expectations=2C they=E2=
=80=99re setting those entrepreneurs up for failure and shareholder revolt=
 when the bubble bursts; but without that exuberance=2C we wouldn=E2=80=99=
t be able to move forward much at all. Alexander Siemens describes this dy=
namic in =E2=80=9CThe Brush Broom=E2=80=9D=2C writing about the speculativ=
e bubble around electric lighting at the turn of last century:

=E2=80=9CHowever much other causes may have contributed to delay the devel=
opment of electrical engineering=2C it is clear that the principal must be=

Coming back to Silicon Valley=2C we can recognize many different component=

=80=99t ever use the word promoter because in a sense=2C we=E2=80=99re all=
 doing that job. Founders=2C VCs=2C marketers and thought leaders galore a=

sue. But there=E2=80=99s another reason why I completely get why the term=
 has vanished=2C and that=E2=80=99s a peculiarity of the modern tech indus=
try that often goes underappreciated by many of its participants: equity s=
hares of startups aren=E2=80=99t freely traded. When investors buy shares=
 in a startup in a funding round=2C it=E2=80=99s to keep them - not to fli=

oading their shares onto those with less
information. So in that sense=2C this second core function of a promoter -=
 to move product - is almost entirely absent from Silicon Valley=2C minus=

But you know where that=E2=80=99s most definitely not the case? In the cry=
ptocurrency community=2C where issued tokens and coins can be freely excha=
nged on liquid exchanges=2C as if they were publicly traded securities=2C=
 whether the law agrees or not. And=2C sure enough=2C that job of the =E2=
=80=9Cpromoter=E2=80=9D has become roaring back: =E2=80=9CICO Promoter=E2=
=80=9D has fully reemerged as part of the crypto lexicon=2C highly reminis=
cent of the dot com bubble when those same kinds of people promoted intern=
et IPOs to an eager public. It=E2=80=99s been a great story of =E2=80=9Cbe=
 careful what you wish for=E2=80=9D=2C as one of the biggest rallying crie=
s on the way up had been=2C =E2=80=9Cthink about how much innovation we=E2=
=80=99ll be able to fund with a fully liquid and public market for ICOs!=
=E2=80=9D It makes you really appreciate that in Silicon Valley=E2=80=99s=
 privately-held=2C promoter-less model of funding innovation=2C there are=
 remarkably few outright scams. But in the innovation economy=E2=80=99s pa=
st=2C and undoubtedly in its future as well=2C scams have always played a=
 central role=2C
both on the way up and on the way down=2C in the bubbles that have moved t=
he world forward in fits and lurches. Next week=2C we=E2=80=99ll go throug=
h some of the most common kinds of scams that we saw in past centuries in=
